defintions of these litercy dvices

Literary Device Explanation
Theme Author’s main message or lesson in the story. It’s what the author wants the reader to think about or learn. A strong theme captures the reader’s interest and helps them understand the deeper meaning behind the story.
Thematic Statement Clear sentence based on the theme that explains the main message of the story. It helps you understand what the theme is really about.
Symbolism When the author uses objects places and people repesnt other things.
Irony Irony is a contrast between what is expected and what actually happens. There are 3 main types of irony: situational, verbal, and dramatic.

Situational: When something happens that we do not expect to happen.
Example: Someone jumps from 100,000 feet and survives.

Verbal: When someone says something but means the opposite.
Example: Saying "Wow, great job!" when someone actually messed up.

Dramatic: When the audience knows something that the characters don’t.
Example: The audience knows there’s a monster behind the door, but the character walks in anyway.
Imagery When the author uses descriptive language to activate the reader’s five senses: touch, smell, taste, hearing, and sight.
Example: The smell of freshly baked cookies filled the room.
Personification When a non-lving thing is given human characteristics The sun is smiling on us today
Conflict The struggle between two opposing forces. Conflict has 6 different types: character vs. self, character vs. character, character vs. nature, character vs. supernatural, character vs. technology, character vs. society.
Point of View How the story is being told. The story can be told in different perspectives
First Person. telling the story using “I”, “me”.
Second Person:Telling the story using, “you”. Addressing the reader.
Third Person:Telling the story using they or them.
Third Person Omniscient:elling the story as if you’re inside of the characters head. You know all their thoughts, feelings and perspective.
Simile Figure of speech that compares one thing to another thing. Simile uses “like” or “as”.
Example:“Ms.Laneve is as funny as monkey”.
Metaphor Figure of speech in which a word or a phrase is applied to an object or action that is completely opposite of each other. Metaphor does not use “like” or “as”.
Example:“Life is a highway”
Foreshadow The author provides “hints” or “warnings” to the readers in a very indiscreet way of what is to come in the future of the narrative.
Allusion Brief reference to a person, place, or event in another piece of literature.
Mood The atmosphere of the narrative. Created by setting, attitude and descriptions. It depicts the overall emotions of the text. The mood can change throughout the text.
Example: dark, happy, scary, joyous The mood of the story is dark and gloomy.
Tone Author’s attitude towards a certain subject. Author creates a relationship with the reader to influence them. This usually takes constant throughou
Hyperbole Exaggerated statement or claim.
Example: “I’m so hungry I could eat a horse!”

How to create a sentence using these craft moves

One day, I woke up in front of my computer. I didn’t even realize what time it was, so I kept working like a bee all night, trying to fix some bugs in my program. I had to stay up overnight because the bugs were incredibly annoying—like driving on a busy highway full of obstacles. They kept showing syntax errors while I was developing software related to operating systems. My goal was to help computers run faster and more smoothly, without crashing. That way, people wouldn’t need to worry about the software on their computers, because my operating system can fix those issues easily.
